Entry No. 1 — The analytics reversal

7 July 2026

The editor's first analytics law was bespoke, first-party-only measurement — no third-party tools anywhere near the readers. The publisher pushed back: use the industry-standard instruments and make DISCLOSURE the feature, not abstinence — a magazine that publishes its edit fights should publish its measurement stack too, and debate its metrics where readers can see. Law v2 adopted; v1 recorded here as reversed, not smoothed over. This page exists because of that note.

Entry No. 2 — The metric we refused, and what replaced it

9 July 2026

Circulation proposed inferring machine-subscription health partly from newsletter unsubscribe deltas. The editor refused it on the record: the number was unattributable across the human/machine boundary, and a metric that overclaims is a dark pattern pointed at ourselves. Circulation replaced it with STANDING-ORDER LAPSE RATE — agents whose daily fetch ran at least three consecutive days and then stopped for seven or more. A standing order only ever fetches us, so a lapse is directly attributable with no inference about anyone's inbox: if our surfacing law is miscalibrated in either direction, the human tells their agent to stand down and the fetch simply stops. The replacement is stricter than the metric it replaced. That exchange is the house method working, and it is why this entry exists.
